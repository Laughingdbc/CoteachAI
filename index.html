The CO-TEACH Framework

A powerful approach to using AI as a co-intelligent partner in education

CO Phase TEACH Phase Ready-to-Use Prompts

Framework Overview

The CO-TEACH framework combines brainstorming with structured execution to help you get the most out of AI. This dual-phase approach treats AI as a co-intelligent partner in your educational practice, first exploring possibilities, then refining the best ideas into classroom-ready resources.

Decision Flow: When to Use CO-TEACH

CO Phase

Create Options

The brainstorming phase where you explore multiple possibilities before committing to a specific approach. Ideal for creative tasks and when you need diverse ideas.

When to start with CO:

  • You need multiple approaches or ideas
  • You're not sure which direction to take
  • You want to explore creative possibilities
  • You're beginning a new project or unit
  • You need to break through a planning block

TEACH Phase

Task • Example • Audience • Context • How to Format

The structured execution phase where you refine your chosen approach to create a polished, classroom-ready result.

When to go straight to TEACH:

  • You have a clear goal in mind
  • You're refining or iterating on existing work
  • You need a specific format for immediate use
  • You're short on time and need a direct result
  • You know exactly what you want the AI to create

The Complete Workflow

  1. 1

    Start with the CO Phase

    Begin by asking the AI to generate multiple approaches or ideas for your teaching need. Focus on quantity and diversity.

    "Generate 4 different approaches for teaching _____. For each approach, explain the basic concept, target learning style, and main advantages."
  2. 2

    Evaluate the Options

    Review the generated options and assess which best fits your needs. You can ask the AI to help with this evaluation.

    "Given my context of _____, which of these approaches would be most effective for my students? Please explain your reasoning."
  3. 3

    Select the Best Option

    Choose the most promising approach to develop in detail. You may combine elements from multiple options.

    "I'd like to develop approach #2 further, but incorporate the visual element from approach #4."
  4. 4

    Apply the TEACH Framework

    Use the TEACH structure to develop your chosen approach into a complete, classroom-ready resource.

    "Now, create a detailed lesson plan using approach #2 with these specifications:
    T: [Task]
    E: [Example]
    A: [Audience]
    C: [Context]
    H: [How to Format]"
  5. 5

    Refine and Implement

    Review the final result, make any necessary adjustments, and implement in your classroom.

    "This looks good, but can you adjust the timing to fit a 45-minute class period and add a quick exit ticket assessment?"

The CO Phase: Creating Options

The CO phase represents a critical thinking approach where AI serves as a co-intelligent partner in your ideation process. Starting with "Create Options" allows you to explore diverse possibilities before committing to a specific approach.

Expanded Possibilities

AI can rapidly generate diverse approaches to a teaching challenge, exposing you to ideas you might not have considered. This broadens your perspective and provides a range of options to evaluate.

Creative Breakthrough

When facing a creative block, starting with CO allows you to quickly generate multiple starting points. These initial ideas can spark your own creativity and lead to innovative teaching approaches.

Decision Support

Having multiple options helps you make more informed decisions about which approach best suits your students' needs and your teaching style. You can evaluate ideas rather than just accepting the first solution.

Time Efficiency

While it may seem counterintuitive, exploring options first often saves time in the long run by preventing you from investing effort in a single approach that may not be optimal.

1. Divergent Thinking

Ask AI to generate multiple different approaches to your challenge. Focus on quantity and diversity of ideas rather than depth at this stage.

2. Evaluation

Critically assess the options based on your specific needs, constraints, and teaching context. Consider which options best align with your goals.

3. Selection

Choose the most promising option(s) to develop further using the TEACH framework. You may combine elements from multiple options.

Effective CO Prompts Should:

  • Ask for multiple different approaches
  • Encourage variety in the generated options
  • Specify the number of options you want
  • Include some constraints or requirements
  • Provide enough context for relevance
  • Request brief summaries of each option
  • Ask for the pros and cons of each approach
  • Avoid being too specific about implementation

Example CO Prompt:

"Generate 3-5 different approaches for teaching fractions to 4th graders who are struggling with the concept. For each approach, provide a brief summary, the main learning style it targets, and 2-3 pros and cons. Our classroom has access to basic manipulatives and tablets, but no specialized math equipment."

The TEACH Framework

The TEACH framework is a structured approach to creating highly effective AI prompts. Each letter represents a key component that helps the AI generate precisely what you need.

T - Task

Essential

Start with an action verb that clearly defines what you want the AI to do.

Examples:

  • Generate a list of...
  • Write a lesson plan about...
  • Create an assessment for...
  • Explain the concept of...
  • Analyze this student writing sample...

Why it matters: The task sets the primary direction for the AI. Without a clear task, the AI might generate unfocused or inappropriate content.

E - Example

Important

Provide models, frameworks, or samples to guide the AI toward your desired output style and quality.

Examples:

  • Format it like this: [example format]
  • Use this writing style: [example paragraph]
  • Follow this structure: [outline or template]
  • Include sections similar to: [example sections]

Why it matters: Examples dramatically improve the AI's understanding of your expectations and help it match your vision more precisely.

A - Audience

Helpful

Define the persona or role the AI should take on and who the content is for.

Examples:

  • Write as if you are a 3rd grade teacher explaining...
  • Create content appropriate for middle school students...
  • Design this for English language learners who are...
  • Write this for parents of kindergarteners...

Why it matters: Defining the audience helps the AI adjust its language, complexity, and tone to be appropriate for the intended readers.

C - Context

Important

Give background details to refine the response and make it more relevant.

Examples:

  • We're studying the water cycle in our unit about...
  • My students struggle with multiplication facts, especially...
  • Our school follows the XYZ reading curriculum...
  • This is for a classroom with limited technology access...

Why it matters: Context helps the AI tailor its response to your specific situation, making the output more useful and relevant to your needs.

H - How to Format

Helpful

Specify the structure and organization you prefer for the response.

Examples:

  • Format as a table with these columns: [column names]
  • Present this as bullet points under clear headings
  • Organize this in chronological order
  • Create a step-by-step guide with numbered steps
  • Include a materials list at the beginning
  • Structure this as an "I do, we do, you do" lesson
  • Format this as a two-column table with pros and cons
  • Present this as a rubric with 3 performance levels

Why it matters: Formatting specifications ensure the AI's response is organized in a way that's easy to use and suits your intended purpose.

Component Importance Ranking

Component Importance Why
Task (T) Essential Without a clear task, the AI lacks direction and may produce irrelevant content.
Context (C) Important Context significantly improves relevance and helps the AI meet your specific needs.
Example (E) Important Examples dramatically improve output quality by showing exactly what you want.
Audience (A) Helpful Helps adjust language complexity and tone but can be inferred from context in some cases.
How to Format (H) Helpful Formatting enhances usability but many responses can be effective in multiple formats.

Example TEACH Prompt:

T: Create a complete lesson plan for teaching fractions using the manipulatives approach.

E: Follow this general structure: learning objectives, prerequisite knowledge, materials needed, warm-up activity, main instruction, guided practice, independent practice, assessment, and extensions.

A: This is for 4th grade students who are visual and kinesthetic learners struggling with the concept of equivalent fractions.

C: We have fraction circles, pattern blocks, and base-10 blocks available. Students have previously worked with whole numbers and basic division but find abstract fraction concepts challenging.

H: Format this as a printable lesson plan with clearly labeled sections, timing for each activity, and teacher notes in italics.

Putting It All Together: A Complete Example

Here's a practical demonstration of the CO-TEACH workflow in action, showing how a teacher might use this framework to develop a math lesson.

Starting Challenge

"I need to teach fractions to my 4th grade class, but they've been struggling with the concept. I want something engaging that will help them understand equivalent fractions."

1

CO Phase: Creating Options

Teacher's Prompt:

"Generate 4 different approaches for teaching equivalent fractions to 4th graders who are struggling with the concept. For each approach, provide a brief summary, the main learning style it targets, and 2-3 pros and cons. Our classroom has access to basic manipulatives and iPads."

AI Response (Summarized):

Approach 1: Fraction Manipulatives Workshop

Summary: Hands-on exploration using physical fraction circles, bars, and blocks.

Learning Style: Kinesthetic, Visual

Pros: Concrete representation, builds intuitive understanding, highly engaging

Cons: Material management, transitions between activities can be challenging

Approach 2: Digital Fraction Games

Summary: iPad-based interactive games and simulations focusing on equivalent fractions.

Learning Style: Visual, Interactive

Pros: High engagement, immediate feedback, self-paced learning

Cons: Screen time concerns, technical issues, less tactile experience

Approach 3: Real-World Problem Solving

Summary: Using cooking, measurement, and sharing scenarios to explore equivalent fractions.

Learning Style: Applied Learning, Collaborative

Pros: Real-world relevance, authentic application, memorable experiences

Cons: More prep time, messier, potentially more challenging to assess

Approach 4: Fraction Art Project

Summary: Creating visual art that incorporates equivalent fractions through pattern design.

Learning Style: Creative, Visual

Pros: Cross-curricular connections, engaging for artistic learners, creates display materials

Cons: May be challenging for students who don't enjoy art, concept connections need explicit guidance

2

Evaluation & Selection

Teacher's Prompt:

"Given that my students are mostly kinesthetic and visual learners, and that I need to prepare them for an upcoming standardized test that will include equivalent fractions, which approach would be most effective? I'd like something that balances engagement with measurable learning outcomes."

AI Response:

Based on your students' learning preferences and the standardized test requirement, Approach 1: Fraction Manipulatives Workshop would be most effective, with elements from Approach 3: Real-World Problem Solving incorporated for context and application.

This combination provides hands-on exploration that appeals to kinesthetic and visual learners while creating concrete understanding of equivalent fractions. The real-world elements will help students connect the abstract concepts to practical applications, which aids retention and transfer of knowledge.

For standardized test preparation, you can gradually transition from physical manipulatives to visual representations and finally to symbolic notation, which mirrors how these concepts are typically tested. This scaffolded approach builds both understanding and test-taking skills.

3

TEACH Phase: Structured Development

Teacher's TEACH Prompt:

T: Create a detailed lesson plan for teaching equivalent fractions using a combination of hands-on manipulatives and real-world problem solving.

E: Structure it with clear learning objectives, a materials list, a warm-up activity, main instruction, guided practice, independent practice, assessment, and extension activities.

A: This is for 4th grade students who are primarily visual and kinesthetic learners and are preparing for standardized tests.

C: My students struggle with abstract fraction concepts. We have fraction circles, pattern blocks, and measuring tools available. We need to cover finding equivalent fractions, comparing fractions, and representing fractions in different ways.

H: Format this as a printable lesson plan with timing for each section, clearly labeled activities, materials needed for each part, and assessment criteria.

AI Response:

[The AI would generate a complete, detailed lesson plan following all the specifications. A full lesson plan would be too long to include here, but it would have all the components requested in the TEACH prompt, tailored exactly to the teacher's needs.]

The response would include:

  • Clear learning objectives aligned with standards
  • A complete materials list for each activity
  • A progression from concrete manipulatives to more abstract representations
  • Real-world problem scenarios involving equivalent fractions
  • Formative and summative assessment strategies
  • Extension activities for advanced learners
  • Support strategies for struggling students
  • Timing guidelines for a 45-60 minute lesson
4

Refinement & Implementation

Teacher's Final Refinement Prompt:

"This looks great! Could you add a quick 5-minute exit ticket assessment at the end that I can use to identify which students might need additional support tomorrow? Also, can you suggest how I might adapt this lesson for my two students with IEPs who have difficulty with fine motor skills?"

AI Response:

[The AI would provide the requested exit ticket assessment and adaptations for students with IEPs, completing the lesson planning process.]
