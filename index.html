The CO-TEACH Framework

A powerful approach to using AI as a co-intelligent partner in education

Framework Overview

The CO-TEACH framework combines brainstorming with structured execution to help you get the most out of AI. This dual-phase approach treats AI as a co-intelligent partner in your educational practice, first exploring possibilities, then refining the best ideas into classroom-ready resources.

Decision Flow: When to Use CO-TEACH

CO Phase

Create Options

The brainstorming phase where you explore multiple possibilities before committing to a specific approach. Ideal for creative tasks and when you need diverse ideas.

When to start with CO:

  • You need multiple approaches or ideas
  • You're not sure which direction to take
  • You want to explore creative possibilities
  • You're beginning a new project or unit
  • You need to break through a planning block

TEACH Phase

Task • Example • Audience • Context • How to Format

The structured execution phase where you refine your chosen approach to create a polished, classroom-ready result.

When to go straight to TEACH:

  • You have a clear goal in mind
  • You're refining or iterating on existing work
  • You need a specific format for immediate use
  • You're short on time and need a direct result
  • You know exactly what you want the AI to create

The Complete Workflow

  1. 1

    Start with the CO Phase

    Begin by asking the AI to generate multiple approaches or ideas for your teaching need. Focus on quantity and diversity.

    "Generate 4 different approaches for teaching _____. For each approach, explain the basic concept, target learning style, and main advantages."
  2. 2

    Evaluate the Options

    Review the generated options and assess which best fits your needs. You can ask the AI to help with this evaluation.

    "Given my context of _____, which of these approaches would be most effective for my students? Please explain your reasoning."
  3. 3

    Select the Best Option

    Choose the most promising approach to develop in detail. You may combine elements from multiple options.

    "I'd like to develop approach #2 further, but incorporate the visual element from approach #4."
  4. 4

    Apply the TEACH Framework

    Use the TEACH structure to develop your chosen approach into a complete, classroom-ready resource.

    "Now, create a detailed lesson plan using approach #2 with these specifications:
    T: [Task]
    E: [Example]
    A: [Audience]
    C: [Context]
    H: [How to Format]"
  5. 5

    Refine and Implement

    Review the final result, make any necessary adjustments, and implement in your classroom.

    "This looks good, but can you adjust the timing to fit a 45-minute class period and add a quick exit ticket assessment?"

The CO Phase: Creating Options

The CO phase represents a critical thinking approach where AI serves as a co-intelligent partner in your ideation process. Starting with "Create Options" allows you to explore diverse possibilities before committing to a specific approach.

Expanded Possibilities

AI can rapidly generate diverse approaches to a teaching challenge, exposing you to ideas you might not have considered. This broadens your perspective and provides a range of options to evaluate.

Creative Breakthrough

When facing a creative block, starting with CO allows you to quickly generate multiple starting points. These initial ideas can spark your own creativity and lead to innovative teaching approaches.

Decision Support

Having multiple options helps you make more informed decisions about which approach best suits your students' needs and your teaching style. You can evaluate ideas rather than just accepting the first solution.

Time Efficiency

While it may seem counterintuitive, exploring options first often saves time in the long run by preventing you from investing effort in a single approach that may not be optimal.

1. Divergent Thinking

Ask AI to generate multiple different approaches to your challenge. Focus on quantity and diversity of ideas rather than depth at this stage.

2. Evaluation

Critically assess the options based on your specific needs, constraints, and teaching context. Consider which options best align with your goals.

3. Selection

Choose the most promising option(s) to develop further using the TEACH framework. You may combine elements from multiple options.

Effective CO Prompts Should:

  • Ask for multiple different approaches
  • Encourage variety in the generated options
  • Specify the number of options you want
  • Include some constraints or requirements
  • Provide enough context for relevance
  • Request brief summaries of each option
  • Ask for the pros and cons of each approach
  • Avoid being too specific about implementation

Example CO Prompt:

"Generate 3-5 different approaches for teaching fractions to 4th graders who are struggling with the concept. For each approach, provide a brief summary, the main learning style it targets, and 2-3 pros and cons. Our classroom has access to basic manipulatives and tablets, but no specialized math equipment."

The TEACH Framework

The TEACH framework is a structured approach to creating highly effective AI prompts. Each letter represents a key component that helps the AI generate precisely what you need.

T - Task

Essential

Start with an action verb that clearly defines what you want the AI to do.

Examples:

  • Generate a list of...
  • Write a lesson plan about...
  • Create an assessment for...
  • Explain the concept of...
  • Analyze this student writing sample...

Why it matters: The task sets the primary direction for the AI. Without a clear task, the AI might generate unfocused or inappropriate content.

E - Example

Important

Provide models, frameworks, or samples to guide the AI toward your desired output style and quality.

Examples:

  • Format it like this: [example format]
  • Use this writing style: [example paragraph]
  • Follow this structure: [outline or template]
  • Include sections similar to: [example sections]

Why it matters: Examples dramatically improve the AI's understanding of your expectations and help it match your vision more precisely.

A - Audience

Helpful

Define the persona or role the AI should take on and who the content is for.

Examples:

  • Write as if you are a 3rd grade teacher explaining...
  • Create content appropriate for middle school students...
  • Design this for English language learners who are...
  • Write this for parents of kindergarteners...

Why it matters: Defining the audience helps the AI adjust its language, complexity, and tone to be appropriate for the intended readers.

C - Context

Important

Give background details to refine the response and make it more relevant.

Examples:

  • We're studying the water cycle in our unit about...
  • My students struggle with multiplication facts, especially...
  • Our school follows the XYZ reading curriculum...
  • This is for a classroom with limited technology access...

Why it matters: Context helps the AI tailor its response to your specific situation, making the output more useful and relevant to your needs.

H - How to Format

Helpful

Specify the structure and organization you prefer for the response.

Examples:

  • Format as a table with these columns: [column names]
  • Present this as bullet points under clear headings
  • Organize this in chronological order
  • Create a step-by-step guide with numbered steps
  • Include a materials list at the beginning
  • Structure this as an "I do, we do, you do" lesson
  • Format this as a two-column table with pros and cons
  • Present this as a rubric with 3 performance levels

Why it matters: Formatting specifications ensure the AI's response is organized in a way that's easy to use and suits your intended purpose.

Component Importance Ranking

Component Importance Why
Task (T) Essential Without a clear task, the AI lacks direction and may produce irrelevant content.
Context (C) Important Context significantly improves relevance and helps the AI meet your specific needs.
Example (E) Important Examples dramatically improve output quality by showing exactly what you want.
Audience (A) Helpful Helps adjust language complexity and tone but can be inferred from context in some cases.
How to Format (H) Helpful Formatting enhances usability but many responses can be effective in multiple formats.

Level 1/5: Basic Prompt (T only)

Scenario: Ms. Rivera, a 4th grade teacher, wants to create a science activity about the water cycle

Level 1/5: Basic Prompt (T only)

Basic Prompt (T only)

"Create a water cycle activity."

Ms. Rivera is just starting to use AI tools

AI Response

Water Cycle Activity

Here's a water cycle activity you can use in your classroom:

  1. Fill a clear container with water
  2. Cover it with plastic wrap
  3. Place a weight in the center of the plastic wrap
  4. Put the container in sunlight
  5. Observe the water evaporating and condensing

This demonstrates evaporation, condensation, and precipitation in a simple model.

Problem: This generic activity doesn't specify grade level, time requirements, or learning objectives. It's unclear if students already know the water cycle concepts or what materials are available.

Level 2/5: Better Prompt (T + C)

Better Prompt (T + C)

"Create a water cycle activity. This is for 4th graders who have already learned the basic stages of the water cycle. We have limited materials and only 30 minutes for the activity."

Ms. Rivera adds context about her students and situation

AI Response

30-Minute Water Cycle Review Activity for 4th Grade

Since your students already know the basic stages, here's a quick review activity that uses minimal materials:

Materials:

  • Index cards or small paper slips
  • Pencils and colored markers
  • Tape

Activity:

  1. Divide students into small groups
  2. Each group creates cards representing different stages of the water cycle
  3. Students arrange cards in sequence and explain connections
  4. Groups share with the class

Better, but: The activity is more appropriate for the time and age level, but it's still rather generic. It doesn't address specific learning objectives or student engagement preferences.

Level 3/5: Strong Prompt (T + C + A)

Strong Prompt (T + C + A)

"Create a water cycle activity. This is for 4th graders who have already learned the basic stages of the water cycle. We have limited materials and only 30 minutes for the activity. My students are hands-on learners who enjoy collaborative group work."

Ms. Rivera adds information about her audience's learning preferences

AI Response

Collaborative Water Cycle Stations for Hands-on Learners

This 30-minute activity leverages your 4th graders' preference for hands-on, collaborative learning:

Materials:

  • 4 plastic cups per group
  • Blue food coloring or washable markers
  • Cotton balls
  • Paper towels
  • Water

Activity:

  1. Arrange students in groups of 3-4
  2. Each group creates a mini water cycle demonstration across their four cups
  3. Students add colored water to first cup (ocean), use cotton for clouds, and observe "rain" through paper towel transfer
  4. Groups discuss how their model represents each stage of the water cycle

Much improved: The activity matches the students' learning style with hands-on collaboration. It's appropriately timed and uses accessible materials, but still lacks a clear format and curricular connections.

Level 4/5: Excellent Prompt (T + C + A + H)

Excellent Prompt (T + C + A + H)

"Create a water cycle activity. This is for 4th graders who have already learned the basic stages of the water cycle. We have limited materials and only 30 minutes for the activity. My students are hands-on learners who enjoy collaborative group work. Structure this as a bullet-pointed list with sections for objective, materials, step-by-step instructions, and assessment questions."

Ms. Rivera specifies the exact format she needs

AI Response

Water Cycle Challenge: Collaborative Exploration

• Objective:

  • Students will demonstrate understanding of water cycle processes through a hands-on collaborative activity
  • Students will make connections between water cycle stages using a physical model

• Materials:

  • Clear plastic cups (4 per group)
  • Blue food coloring or washable markers
  • Cotton balls (5-6 per group)
  • Paper towels
  • Small cups of water
  • Sticky notes (4 colors if possible)

• Step-by-Step Instructions:

  1. Divide class into groups of 3-4 students (5 minutes)
  2. Distribute materials to each group (3 minutes)
  3. Groups create water cycle model following guided instructions (15 minutes)
  4. Groups label each part of their model with the correct water cycle term (3 minutes)
  5. Quick group sharing of one insight from their model (4 minutes)

• Assessment Questions:

  • What stage of the water cycle does each part of your model represent?
  • How does water change form throughout the cycle?
  • What might happen if one stage of the water cycle was disrupted?
  • How does this model help you understand the real water cycle?

Excellent: The activity is now perfectly structured for classroom use with clear timing, learning objectives, materials, instructions, and assessment questions—exactly in the requested format.

Level 5/5: Expert Prompt (All T.E.A.C.H. Elements)

Expert Prompt (All T.E.A.C.H. Elements)

"Create a water cycle activity. I did a similar activity last year where students created 'mini terrariums' in plastic cups with lids to observe condensation and evaporation, but I want something new this year. My students are hands-on learners who enjoy collaborative group work. This is for 4th graders who have already learned the basic stages of the water cycle. We have limited materials and only 30 minutes for the activity. We're focusing on how the water cycle impacts local ecosystems. Structure this as a bullet-pointed list with sections for objective, materials, step-by-step instructions, and assessment questions."

Ms. Rivera has mastered the T.E.A.C.H. framework

AI Response

Ecosystem Water Journey: Interactive Watershed Modeling

• Objective:

  • Students will connect water cycle knowledge to local ecosystem impacts through a collaborative watershed model
  • Students will predict and observe how water travels through different environments
  • Students will identify how disruptions to the water cycle affect local habitats

• Materials:

  • Aluminum foil (1 large sheet per group)
  • Washable markers (blue, green, brown)
  • Small spray bottles with water
  • Sponge pieces (2-3 per group)
  • Small cups or containers (3 per group)
  • Ecosystem picture cards (forest, city, farm, river, ocean)

• Step-by-Step Instructions:

  1. Arrange students in groups of 3-4 (3 minutes)
  2. Each group creates a 3D watershed model using crumpled then partially flattened aluminum foil in a shallow container (5 minutes)
  3. Groups use markers to add ecosystem features to their watershed—forests (sponges), urban areas, farmland, rivers leading to a collection area (10 minutes)
  4. Students use spray bottles to simulate rain over their watershed and observe water flow (5 minutes)
  5. Groups discuss how water moved through each ecosystem and what pollutants might be collected in each area (5 minutes)
  6. Quick sharing of one ecosystem impact observation (2 minutes)

• Assessment Questions:

  • How did water change as it moved through different ecosystems in your model?
  • What happened when rain fell on the forest areas (sponges) compared to urban areas?
  • How might drought affect each ecosystem in your watershed?
  • What local ecosystems in our area are most affected by the water cycle?
  • How does this model help us understand the connection between the water cycle and ecosystem health?

Extension: If time permits, students can add small amounts of colored water to represent pollution sources and observe how contaminants travel through the watershed.

Exceptional: The AI provided an innovative activity that builds on previous experience, meets curriculum needs by connecting water cycle to ecosystems, uses an engaging approach perfect for collaborative hands-on learners, and includes everything needed for immediate classroom implementation.

What This Demonstrates:

  • Even a basic prompt (Task only) can get a response, but it's generic and requires significant adaptation.
  • Adding Context significantly improves relevance to your specific situation.
  • Including Audience helps tailor the response to your students' needs and preferences.
  • Specifying How to Format makes the response immediately more usable.
  • Providing an Example dramatically improves the AI's understanding of your expectations and helps it generate more innovative alternatives.
